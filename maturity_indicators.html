Indicators of Maturity

We are citing a number of different pieces of research about maturity of delivery. These include:

State of DevOps Report

  • This report, which surveyed 1900 technical professionals worldwide, found that job satisfaction is #1 predictor of profitability, market share, and productivity
  • It found a strong correlation between the maturity of an organisation and the amount of time it would take them to release a single line of code into production. High performing organisations reported that this would take less than hour.
  • This finding shows the importance of techniques like Continuous Delivery, and of releasing with smaller batch sizes through automated pipelines.
  • The mantra that the report endorses is: “If it hurts, do it more frequently”. In other words, if an organisation's deployment processes are long, painful and fragile, more frequent deployments will help to improve and streamline those processes.
  • Deployments that are easier and more frequent will also allow organisations to experiment. This allows them to take a scientific approach to finding out which features work, through concepts like A/B testing.
  • The report also found that outsourcing is a negative indicator of maturity in organisations.
  • The Westrum Organisational Culture research is also referenced as a strong positive indicator of organisational maturity. This research emphasises the importance of a culture where new ideas are welcomed and failures are seen as opportunities to learn.

Google reWork Report

  • Google's research into their own teams looked into the predictors of high performance. The research found that five key dynamics set successful teams apart from other teams:
    1. Psychological safety: "Can we take risks on this team without feeling insecure or embarrassed?"
    2. Dependability: "Can we count on each other to do high quality work on time?"
    3. Structure & clarity: "Are goals, roles, and execution plans on our team clear?"
    4. Meaning of work: "Are we working on something that is personally important for each of us?"
    5. Impact of work: "Do we fundamentally believe that the work we’re doing matters?"

Stripe - The Developer Coefficient

  • This report looks into the economic impacts of inefficiencies in the developer ecosystem. The senior executives who were surveyed reported that access to developers was the biggest threat to their organisations - more important to them than access to capital.
  • The report estimated a $300 billion annual loss from developer inefficiency, including:
    • Maintenance of legacy systems and technical debt
    • Leadership prioritisation
    • Building custom technology

Incidents - Trends from the Trenches

Subbu Allamaraju, Vice President of Technology at Expedia, studied several hundred production incidents from a large set of customer-facing applications and services running both on-premises and in the cloud. There are a number of lessons that he suggests can be learned from this research:

  • Change safety, including:
    • Progressive deployment methods, like canary releases
    • Feature flags
    • Blue/green deployments
    • Predictable rollbacks
    • Shadow testing
  • Fault containment/redundancy, including:
    • Redundant copies
    • Compartmentalisation
    • Low coupling
  • Reducing undifferentiated heavy lifting or toil through the use of automation and cloud services
  • Observability of systems
  • Importance of post-incident rituals, such as blameless post-mortems
