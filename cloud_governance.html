Cloud Governance

Cloud-Specific Risks

There are three risks that are specifically associated with using cloud:

  • Cost
    • The self-service nature of cloud raises budgetary concerns. Developers and engineers can create resources that can incur sudden cost increases, leading to bill shock. Similarly, agencies must be active in terminating instances that are not in use - projects that are completed, or development systems that aren't in use out-of-hours.
    • Cloud's ability to scale dynamically can have budgetary impacts. This could include auto-scaling compute instances, data egress fees, or unexpected increases in data holdings.
  • Security
    • The self-service nature of cloud means that developers or engineers can inadvertently inject security vulnerabilities into services.
    • There are specific risks related to the level of access the provider's employees have to your systems. Note that an agency's relationship with a cloud service provider could, depending on the provider, be more like a traditional outsourcing arrangement or more like their relationship with the provider of their operating system.
  • Delivery
    • Slow processes that impede agility can be one of the major risks of cloud adoption. As outlined in the Secure Cloud Strategy, one of the key benefits of cloud is agility: business areas can eliminate the lead times that delay delivery, rapidly prototyping and experimenting in order to deliver the best possible service for users.
    • Delivering the wrong thing is, as always, a risk. Cloud provides the ability to prototype rapidly, experiment easily and analyse the results of experiments. But if the research isn't performed to understand user needs, this could simply make for faster delivery of the wrong thing.

Senior Responsible Official

The SRO is the single point of overall accountability for the successful adoption of cloud within the agency. They are accountable for ensuring the agency's cloud program meets the goals of the agency's cloud strategy and of the Government's Secure Cloud Strategy. Typically, the SRO would be an officer at the Senior Executive Service Band 2 or Band 3 level.

Following a DevOps model, the SRO must have aligned incentives: they must have an approach that balances delivery and security, understanding that moving slowly often increases our risk.

Cloud Business Office

The SRO can be complemented by a Cloud Business Office, which ensures that key stakeholders from across the agency are represented. In this model, the SRO would fill the role of Cloud Transformation Leader.

A key role of the Cloud Business Office is to have oversight of the information emerging from the information being radiated from delivery teams. The executives in the Cloud Business Office need to have a sufficient level of understanding of the specific risks of cloud - cost, security and delivery - to be able to interrogate teams about the information they radiate.

Scalable Cloud Governance

The Cloud Business Office leads the adoption of Scalable Cloud Governance to ensure that the specific risks of cloud are governed in a manner that is scalable and agile, ensuring that teams' ability to deliver is minimally impacted by the need to comply with the compliance needs of Government and the agency.

Radiating Information

Teams that are autonomous must not be black boxes. They must radiate information to all stakeholders. This should include information about delivery, security, cost, system health, product usage metrics.

When we talk about ‘radiating information’, we’re not talking about writing reports. That kind of manual reporting can have a strong negative impact on delivery teams. We’re talking about things like kanban boards, which show work in progress and highlight blockers. Or something like Google Analytics, which can reveal insights about user activity. Or the security tools that are built into public cloud providers for owners to get insights in real time into their security posture.

The other essential part of teams radiating information is that leaders must pay attention to this information. They must look at it, and - at some level of granularity - understand it and be able to interrogate it. This needs to be the case at all levels of seniority. Red lights mean nothing if they don’t raise questions.

Removing Obstacles

The Developer Coefficient, a report by Stripe, outlines the rapidly increasing importance of developer productivity. With the agility that cloud provides, it is no longer acceptable for delivery teams to be waiting hours, days or weeks for obstacles to be removed.

As an example, during the deployment of the Australian Bureau of Statistics’ Australian Marriage Law Postal Survey system, the ABS’s Chief Operating Officer was at daily standups to see the work being performed. When blockers were identified, the COO was able to make calls and immediately remove these obstacles. The system was eventually deployed in less than a hundred days. This would be a great achievement in any organisation, but in a public service agency, it is truly exceptional.
